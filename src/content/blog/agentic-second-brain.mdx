## The Problem

Every knowledge management system dies the same way. You start excited. You take notes for a week. Maybe two. Then life gets busy, you stop processing, and your vault becomes a graveyard of unconnected ideas.

The problem isn't discipline. The problem is that traditional <Sidenote>PKM<Fragment slot="note">**Personal Knowledge Management.** The practice of capturing, organizing, and connecting your own information. Most PKM systems assume you'll do the organizing yourself.</Fragment></Sidenote> systems require you to be the librarian, the connector, the reviewer, and the writer. You have to process your own clips. Find your own connections. Review your own goals. That's a lot of cognitive overhead on top of the actual work.

What if the AI did all of that?

Not "AI-assisted note-taking" where you paste text and get a summary. I mean a system where the AI knows your goals, your recurring failures, your vault structure, and it actively works on your knowledge while you sleep, eat, or ship code.

That's what I built.

## The Architecture

The system has three layers:

**Obsidian** is the persistence layer. A vault with 7 folders following a <Sidenote>Zettelkasten<Fragment slot="note">**German for "slip box."** Each note captures one atomic idea and links to others, building a web of knowledge over time. Invented by sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who published 70 books using this method.</Fragment></Sidenote> + <Sidenote>PARA<Fragment slot="note">**Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives.** Tiago Forte's organizational method that sorts information by how actionable it is, not by topic.</Fragment></Sidenote> hybrid:

```
01_Inbox/        -> Raw captures from Web Clipper
02_Journal/      -> Daily logs, weekly plans, reflections
03_Garden/       -> Evergreen atomic notes (concepts, people, meetings)
04_Projects/     -> Active project work
05_Areas/        -> Ongoing life contexts (learning, content, work)
06_Content/      -> Ready-to-publish drafts
07_System/       -> System configuration and context files
```

**Claude Code** is the execution layer. 21 slash commands that process, connect, analyze, and create. Each command reads from the vault, makes decisions, and writes back.

**CLAUDE.md** is the personality layer. It contains your identity, goals, stack preferences, anti-patterns, and communication style. Every time Claude Code starts, it reads this file and knows who you are.

## The Core Loop

Here's what a typical day looks like.

1. **Morning**: I run `/morning`. It pulls my calendar, pending tasks, and recent activity. It gives me three <Sidenote>MITs<Fragment slot="note">**Most Important Things.** The 3 tasks that, if completed, make the day a success regardless of everything else. Set each morning, reviewed each night.</Fragment></Sidenote> for the day. I know exactly what to focus on before I open any code editor.

2. **During the day**: Every time I ship something, `/ship` commits and pushes my code, then logs the commit to my daily journal automatically. If I have a quick thought, `/log "idea about X"` adds a timestamped entry to today's file. No context switching.

3. **Processing**: When my <Sidenote>Obsidian Web Clipper<Fragment slot="note">**Browser extension** that saves web pages directly into your vault as markdown files. Articles, tweets, documentation, anything. It becomes the entry point for all external knowledge.</Fragment></Sidenote> has accumulated articles, I run `/inbox`. It reads each clip, extracts the core ideas, creates atomic notes in `03_Garden/` with proper backlinks, and marks the clips as processed. One command, 10 articles processed in under 3 minutes.

4. **End of day**: `/today` also handles reflection. It audits your planned tasks vs actual, checks MIT progress, integrates health and study data, and asks for a day rating. Carry-over items automatically become tomorrow's planned tasks.

5. **Weekly**: `/pulse` runs a <Sidenote>Pareto analysis<Fragment slot="note">**The 80/20 rule applied to your work.** Finds the 20% of activities producing 80% of your results. `/pulse` ranks your interests by engagement weight and flags the vital few.</Fragment></Sidenote> on my engagement. It reads my daily logs, notes created, and projects touched, then compares them against my stated goals in CLAUDE.md.

## The Anti-Pattern Engine

This is the feature that made someone say "me volaste la cabeza."

Where do these patterns come from? Every year I answer <Sidenote href="https://stephango.com/40-questions">40 questions<Fragment slot="note">**Annual self-reflection exercise** by Steph Ango, CEO of Obsidian. Questions like "What was your biggest failure?" and "What did you waste time on?" force honest pattern recognition across years.</Fragment></Sidenote>. Things like "What was your biggest failure this year?", "What did you waste time on?", "What habit do you keep failing to build?" The answers are brutally honest because nobody reads them except me and my AI. After a few years, the patterns become obvious. Same failures, different year.

So I formalized them. In your CLAUDE.md, you write your recurring failures. Honestly. Not the version you tell recruiters. The real ones:

```markdown
### Sleep Schedule (x2)
**Pattern**: Going to bed late, waking up tired
**Signal**: Average sleep < 7h, commits after midnight
**Intervention**: "Average sleep this week: {X}h. Target is 7+."

### Tutorial Hell (x4)
**Pattern**: Watching tutorials instead of building
**Signal**: 5+ bookmarked courses, 0 new projects started
**Intervention**: "Close the tutorial. Open your editor. Build the thing."
```

The `(xN)` is how many times this pattern has appeared in yearly reviews. The higher the number, the more the system insists.

When you run `/pulse`, it checks these patterns against your actual data. If you haven't exercised in 10 days and your anti-patterns say "No gym consistency (x2)", you'll see:

```
PATTERN ALERT: No exercise logs in 10 days (recurring x2 in reviews)
```

<Callout type="important" title="Why this works">
  Most productivity systems tell you what to do. This one tells you what you keep failing at. There's a difference between a to-do list and a mirror.
</Callout>

Nobody wants to write down their failures. But once you do, and once an AI holds you accountable weekly, the patterns start breaking.

## The /challenge Command

I built <Sidenote>anti-sycophancy<Fragment slot="note">**Making the AI disagree with you.** Most AI assistants default to agreement ("Great idea!"). `/challenge` forces it to argue against your position using your own vault data as evidence.</Fragment></Sidenote> into my knowledge system.

Every AI assistant wants to agree with you. "Great idea!" "That sounds promising!" This is dangerous when you're making big decisions. So I built `/challenge`.

When you run `/challenge "I should learn Rust"`, it searches your vault for evidence, then argues against your position using six analytical lenses:

1. **Opportunity Cost** - What are you NOT doing by learning Rust?
2. **Survivorship Bias** - Are you only seeing Rust success stories?
3. **Sunk Cost** - Would you start this if you hadn't already bought the book?
4. **Contrarian Evidence** - What in your vault contradicts this?
5. **Second-Order Effects** - What if you're right but the consequences are bad?
6. **Anti-Pattern Match** - Does this match a historical failure? (Like "Learning Without Doing x3")

The key is that it uses YOUR data. Not generic arguments. It searches your notes, your daily logs, your previous decisions. If you wrote six months ago that you wanted to focus on TypeScript, and now you're pivoting to Rust, it will find that note and ask you why.

<Callout type="tip" title="When to use /challenge">
  Before any big decision: career moves, project pivots, learning commitments. The 5 minutes it takes might save you 5 months of going in the wrong direction.
</Callout>

## The Graduate Command

You write daily logs. But how many ideas die in those logs? You write "interesting thought about X" on a Tuesday, and by Friday it's buried under 20 other entries.

`/graduate` reads your recent daily logs and extracts ideas that deserve to be permanent notes. It looks for:

- Insights that connect to existing notes in your garden
- Recurring themes across multiple days
- Ideas you mentioned but never developed

Then it creates proper atomic notes in `03_Garden/` with <Sidenote>backlinks<Fragment slot="note">**Bidirectional links between notes.** If note A links to note B, note B automatically knows about note A. This is how isolated ideas find each other over time.</Fragment></Sidenote>, context, and connections to your existing knowledge.

Your daily logs become a mine. `/graduate` is the miner.

## The Learning System

I'm studying math for ML, and I built a learning loop into the system:

1. **Before a lesson**: `/prime linear-algebra` gives you a 3-minute briefing. What you already know, what prerequisites you need, and the key intuitions to watch for. It primes your brain so the lesson sticks.

2. **After a lesson**: `/learn eigenvalues` creates a structured note with the concept, your understanding, review questions, and connections to what you already know. The questions force recall, not recognition.

3. **Next morning**: `/recall` quizzes you from your own notes. <Sidenote>Spaced retrieval<Fragment slot="note">**Testing yourself at increasing intervals.** Harder than re-reading, but retention jumps from ~20% to ~80%. The struggle of recalling is what builds the memory.</Fragment></Sidenote>. Not flashcards from the internet. Questions from YOUR understanding, in YOUR words.

4. **Tracking**: `/study` logs each session to a daily tracker so you can see streaks and gaps.

Spaced repetition built into your knowledge system. Not a separate app.

## Get Started

```bash
git clone https://github.com/Railly/second-brain.git
cd second-brain

# Copy vault to your location
cp -r vault/ ~/my-brain/

# Copy commands
cp -r .claude/ ~/my-brain/.claude/

# Edit CLAUDE.md with your info
cp CLAUDE.md ~/my-brain/CLAUDE.md

# Open in Obsidian, then run Claude Code
cd ~/my-brain && claude
```

First commands to try:

```bash
/log "Setting up my second brain"     # Quick entry
/inbox                                 # Process any clips
/challenge "I should use AI for everything"  # Test the anti-sycophancy engine
```
